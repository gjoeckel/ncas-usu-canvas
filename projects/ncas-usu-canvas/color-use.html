Using color in a document or webpage, such as color-coding data in a graph, is a common way to convey meaning and create visual interest.

Why Color Use Matters

Relying solely on color to convey information can create barriers for students who cannot perceive color differences, such as those with low vision, colorblindness, or visual processing disorders that affect how they perceive and distinguish colors. By providing additional visual or textual cues, you make sure that all students can access and understand the content.

Learning Objectives

  1. Identify the limitations of using color alone to convey meaning.
  2. Describe the benefit of using consistent colors for similar content elements.
  3. Apply accessible color design strategies to ensure all students have access to your content.

Tips for Success

Combine Color with Other Visual Cues

Don’t rely on color alone to convey meaning when creating content like slides, infographics, charts, notes, etc. Instead, pair colors with text labels, icons, and/or patterns to ensure it’s accessible to all students, including those with colorblindness or low vision.

Maintain Color Consistency Across Similar Content

Use the same colors to represent similar types of content or functions throughout your design. This builds user familiarity and reduces confusion by creating predictable visual associations. For example, when giving formative feedback on student work, you might indicate what’s been done well, needs improvement, or is incorrect. To make your comments more accessible, try pairing:

  • Green text with a checkmark for positive comments
  • Blue text with a looping arrow for ideas that need revisiting
  • Purple text with a barred circle for content that is incorrect

Color Use Applied: Teaching Students How to Label Charts

Mr. Wilson teaches digital literacy in his high school. Today, he is teaching his students how to create charts in Tableau. Like 8% of men, Mr. Wilson is colorblind—he has a form of red-green color deficiency called deuteranomaly—so he understands the importance of creating charts that are accessible to colorblind users.

While teaching the class, Mr. Wilson shows students how to choose a colorblind-friendly palette with colors like blue and orange that makes it easier for people with red-green colorblindness to see difference in the chart. He explains that some people have difficulty distinguishing other colors and teaches the students how to use cues like text labels on bar charts and data point markers on line charts.

After the lesson, a student tells Mr. Wilson that he is also colorblind and thanks him for making the content accessible and explaining how to do it and why it matters.
