Alternative text (or alt text) is a textual substitute for the meaning or purpose of non-text content such as an image.

Why Alternative Text Matters

Images are often used to show important information in web pages, documents and presentations. However, not everyone can perceive visual information from images. Adding alt text to describe the purpose of your image is essential to ensuring all students can access the same content. When alternative text is available for an image, assistive technology devices like screen readers or refreshable braille displays can access that written content and read it in place of the image.

Learning Objectives

  1. Describe why alternative text is important to ensure all students have access to your instructional content.
  2. Write clear and helpful alt text for images used in classroom materials.
  3. Determine when to mark an image as decorative to avoid unnecessary distractions.

Tips for Success

Focus on the Image's Purpose

Write alt text based on what the image is meant to convey in the instructional context where it is being used. Think about what the student should understand or experience from the image. Consider different contexts for the following image:

In a 3rd grade science lesson on the life cycle the alt text might be,

“Adult frog resting on a leaf during the final stage of metamorphosis”

While that exact same image used as a prompt for an 8th grade creative writing course might have the following alt text:

“Sleepy green frog dreaming of new adventures”

Be Simple and Precise

Use clear, concise language to describe the image. Remember that everything you write will be read aloud for some users. Avoid overcomplicating the description or adding unnecessary details. As an example, let’s look at the following image used in an assignment about space satellites:

source

An educator may want to make sure that all students have all the detail from this image and in their attempts to be helpful add extra, unnecessary information in the alt text:

“A photograph taken from space showing a view of the western hemisphere of planet earth that features North America in the center. Central America is also visible, as well as the upper part of South America. A vast blue ocean dominates the planet and swirling cloud formations cover most of the land and ocean.”

Instead, the following brief alt text would be more appropriate for the image in most cases:

“Photo of planet earth from space”

Mark Images as Decorative When Appropriate

If the image does not present important information, mark it as decorative to let assistive technologies know that it can skip over that image. For example, the following image of colored pencils at the top of a welcome to class page is purely decorative and doesn’t provide any meaningful information. Include Important Text

If the image includes important text, be sure to also include that text in the alt text so that it is available to all users. For example, an educator is hoping to encourage his students and posted the following image to his online course:

The alt text for the image should include the same text from the image:

“Make this day great!”

Skip “Image of”

Avoid starting alt text with phrases like “image of” or “picture of.” Assistive technologies like screen readers already recognize images and can communicate that as needed to users so there is no need to add that extra, unnecessary information. Alternative Text Applied: Adding Alternative Text to a Study Guide

Mr. Christensen teaches 11th grade AP Physics and is preparing a study guide for an upcoming quiz on Kinetic energy. He is planning on using the following image in a Word document to help students understand the concept:

When creating the document, he remembers Amira in his second hour course who is blind and uses a screen reader to read her digital documents. He wants to make sure that she has access to the same material and remembers there is a way to add alt text to images in Microsoft Word. He selects the image, opens the Picture Format menu, selects Alt Text, and writes the following description:

“Illustration of a roller coaster train of cars descending a steep hill, demonstrating energy conversion as it gains speed downhill.”

When Amira reviews the study guide, her screen reader recognizes there is alt text for the image and reads it aloud. The alt text confirms her understanding of the concept, and she aces the quiz!
